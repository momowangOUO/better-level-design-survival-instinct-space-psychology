## The Short Version

- Level spaces touch survival instincts before players consciously parse them: sightlines, refuge, scale, material, shadow, and height all change how safe or willing to act a player feels.
- The "protagonist weakness problem" is not solved only by stats. Space can give players visible exits, fallback positions, and chances to build control.
- Prospect and refuge need each other. Pure visibility can feel exposed; pure safety drains movement pressure.
- This is a design rewrite based on Totten's article, not a claim that every spatial reading here is proven by psychology experiments.

## Introduction: Why do levels affect instincts?

A great level is more than just a nice-looking space, or just a combination of enemies, props, and doors. It is more like a "survival tension machine": players enter a dangerous but understandable environment, and after seeing risks, finding shelter, judging routes, and overcoming obstacles, they gain the satisfaction of arriving safely, defeating powerful enemies, or receiving rewards.

The reason why this set of experiences is stable and effective is that it borrows from a very ancient psychological mechanism of human beings. We naturally focus on food, water, open views, retreating positions, uncertainty in the dark, heights beneath our feet, and the safety or danger conveyed by walls and surfaces. Game levels can translate these instincts into gameplay: where is worth going, where you may die, where you can hide, where you can see, where you need courage to cross.

The core value of the original article is that it puts "level design" back from pure geometry and task layout to people's physical sense and survival intuition. In other words, level designers are not just arranging space, but organizing the player's sense of security, fear, control, and curiosity.

## 1. From survival needs to gaming pleasure

The most basic human needs include food, water, shelter, physical security and a predictable environment. The rewards, blood recovery points, save points, safe houses, visible exits, climbable platforms, and hiding shadows in the game are essentially gamified versions of these needs.

When the player finds supplies in the dangerous area, he will have the feeling of "I can continue to live"; when the player sees the next safe platform, he will have the feeling of "I can plan the next step"; when the player escapes from the dark room, there will be a tension release. The fun of the game does not always come from ease, but often from "I have taken care of the danger."

Therefore, rewards in level design should not be understood only as numerical gains. A high platform from which to observe the overall situation, a temporary hiding niche, and a shortcut door opened from behind can all be rewards. They reward the player's spatial understanding, not just the items in their backpack.

## 2. "Protagonist's Weakness Problem": Why do players need space help?

![The protagonist’s weakness and ability growth](assets/01-protagonist-problem.png)

Many game protagonists look powerful, but from an operational perspective, the player avatar is often weak. There are more enemies, bosses are larger, environmental information is incomplete, and routes are often blocked. Players win not because their characters are inherently invincible, but because levels constantly provide tools, clues, and learnable spatial structures.

This may explain why platformers, galactic cities, action-adventures, and survival horror all like to push players from weak to strong. The high platforms, pits, obstacles, and enemies in the early rooms seem to tell the player: "You can't freely traverse here yet." When the player acquires new abilities, weapons, keys, or map knowledge, the same space will be reinterpreted. What was once a threat becomes a shortcut, what was once a wall becomes a route, and what was once an invincible enemy becomes a manageable risk.

This change is very important to level design. It shows that "growth" is not just an increase in attack power on the character sheet, but an increase in the player's control over space. A good level should make players feel: I am not getting stronger without thinking, but I am gradually understanding this place.

## 3. Spatial scale: large, small, narrow, and open will all change the sense of body.

![Three types of spatial scale and emotional effects](assets/02-space-scale.png)

Level size is not simply an art or navigation issue, it directly changes the player's perception of their body.

Tight spaces amplify the feeling of oppression. When a corridor is too narrow, it becomes difficult for players to get around enemies, and turning and retreating are slowed down. At this point the enemy is not just a "unit", but more like a moving wall. Survival horror, dungeons, indoor melee combat, and chase sequences often rely on this spatial logic.

Intimate space brings a sense of control. The room is not large, but it is enough for players to observe the boundaries, understand enemy positions, and utilize bunkers and routes. Many teaching rooms, transition rooms, and puzzle rooms should be close to this size because players need to learn the rules in a controlled environment.

Open spaces create outlook and exposure. Players can see far away, but are also easier to see. This scale is commonly used in boss rooms, battlefield atriums, sniper areas, large bridge decks, and squares. The point of them is not just to be "big", but to make the player aware that they are standing in a position where the environment is scrutinizing them.

In the gray box stage, designers can first ask three questions: Can players quickly understand the boundaries here? Does mobility cover key spaces? If an enemy appears, does the player feel like "I can handle it" or "I'm trapped in space"?

## 4. Prospect and Shelter: Players need to see danger and believe that they can survive.

![Advancement Sequence of Prospect and Asylum](assets/03-prospect-refuge.png)

The "prospect and shelter" often said in architecture is very suitable to explain the level experience. Prospect means being able to see the distance and grasp information; shelter means being sheltered, having a way out, and not completely exposing the body. Humans prefer positions where they can observe the environment and are not easily attacked.

In games, this relationship is almost everywhere. A small bunker in front of the door, a window overlooking the enemy, a balcony observing the courtyard from a height, and a preparation room before entering the arena all serve as "shelter points." From here, players observe the exposed area and decide whether to traverse, sneak, engage, or detour next.

If a level has only outlook and no shelter, players will feel that they are forced to be exposed; if there is only shelter and no outlook, players will feel that the space is dull and lacks goals. The truly effective advancement rhythm is usually: safe point, observation, crossing risk, reaching secondary safety point, observing again.

![Shelter translation from architectural space to stealth gameplay](assets/04-architecture-refuge-gameplay.png)

Stealth games especially rely on this structure. Shadows, cabinets, bunkers, pillars, corners, and movable boxes are not simply "hiding spots", but interfaces used by players to understand patrol routes, reset risks, and plan the next step. The clearer the shelter point, the more players dare to take the initiative to cross danger.

But asylum cannot be infinitely stable. A shelter that is stable enough to cost nothing will become a loophole, and players will just wait in place. Good shelter usually has viewing angles, sound costs, time windows, or the possibility of being countered.

## 5. Enemy level will change the shelter density of the space

![Small enemies and bosses correspond to different outlook shelter structures](assets/05-enemy-boss-space.png)

In the same game system, different enemy types require different space densities.

Common enemy areas generally require more platforms, cover, corners, and secondary routes. Players need to use space to break up combat: dodge ranged attacks, move to the side, create distance, and re-enter the fight. This type of space allows players to feel like "I can handle this."

Boss areas are often the opposite. In order to highlight powerful enemies, the space will be deliberately made wider, the shelter will be reduced, and players will be forced to be exposed to the boss's attack range. The design goal here is not to allow players to always have a place to hide, but to allow players to understand the attack rhythm, learn the safety window, and make judgments within limited retreats.

This is why many boss rooms resemble stages: clear boundaries, clear objectives, and limited room for escape. The space here is not to protect players, but to put players and powerful enemies in the same clear conflict field.

## 6. Shadow, occlusion light and atmospheric uncertainty

![Differences in level psychology between Shadow and Shade](assets/06-shadow-shade.png)

Light and shadow are not just the atmosphere of the picture. It changes the player's judgment of what the space means.

Darkness can be roughly divided into two categories. One type is shadows, which hide objects so players don't know if there are enemies, traps, or routes inside. The other type is shading light, which does not completely hide the object, but makes the meaning of the object ambiguous. The former creates fear, the latter creates mystery.

Total darkness is generally not good design. It will turn players from "fear of the unknown" to "annoyed because they can't see it." Effective darkness should retain clues: silhouettes, sound sources, faint spots of light, reflective materials, distant exits, and the rewards of being illuminated. The more players can speculate, the more they engage in imagination.

![Atmospherically Uncertain Tunnel Design](assets/07-atmospheric-ambiguity.png)

The "atmospheric uncertainty" emphasized in the original article can be understood as: the environment gives signals of safety and danger at the same time, making it impossible for players to immediately classify the space. For example, there is a supply box in a tunnel, but there is a shadow gap next to the supply; there is a blue light source in the distance, but there is a sound behind the light source; the corridor seems to lead to the exit, but there are traces of battle on the ground.

This uncertainty is perfect for horror, stealth, exploration, and high-risk areas. It does not rely on sudden scares, but on letting players continue to reason: "Is this a trap?"

## 7. Material Journey: The surface and walls will tell the player how far away from home it is.

![Material progression in the hero’s journey](assets/08-material-journey.png)

The materials in the level will constantly tell the player "whether this is a suitable place to survive." Wood, grass, clear water, warm light and habitable buildings tend to convey security; rocks, cliffs, ruins, wet stone walls, iron doors, pollution, fire and abyss gradually strip away security.

The order of areas in many adventure games coincides with this kind of material journey: starting from villages and forests, passing through caves, mountains, ruins, dungeons, and finally arriving at volcanoes, abyss, dark castles or alienated spaces. The player may not consciously analyze the material, but the body will read the changes.

The value of material design is that it builds anticipation before textual description. When players see wooden houses and wells, they will look forward to supplies, NPCs, or teaching; when they see iron doors, sewage, gravel, and strong contrasting light and shadow, they will begin to prepare for battles or traps.

Therefore, zone difficulty upgrades should not rely solely on enemy health and damage. A more advanced approach is to make materials, sounds, surfaces, light colors, and spatial scales change simultaneously so that players feel that they are leaving a familiar world before entering danger.

## 8. Height: High points are not natural and safe, and abyss are not simply decorative.

![Height can be both shelter and perspective exposure](assets/09-height-prospect-refuge.png)

Height has two opposite effects in levels.

One is the sheltered height. Players stand on high ground and can observe enemies below, plan routes, and launch long-range attacks. This kind of high point usually requires railings, walls, retreats, and countered paths, otherwise it becomes an unsolvable advantage. A healthy high point should be a prize up for grabs, not a position that unilaterally dominates the field.

The other type is prospective risk. Cliff edges, broken bridges, deep pits, and rooftop edges will make players feel exposed and dizzy. The threat here doesn't necessarily come from enemies, but from the terrain itself. Vertical lines, darkness below, falling sounds, narrow-sided platforms, and missing guardrails all reinforce this feeling.

Height design, in particular, needs to be mindful of "fair fears." Players can be afraid of falling, but they should not fail inexplicably because of camera shots, collisions, or unclear boundaries. The danger can be intense, but the message must be readable.

## 9. Put instinctive principles into checkpoint review

![Level Survival Instinct Checklist](assets/10-design-checklist.svg)

If the original ideas are transformed into a practical review form, the level can be checked from five dimensions.

Spatial scale: Does this area feel oppressive, controlling, or exposed to the player? Does this feeling serve current goals?

Outlook and Shelter: Can the player see the next possible safe point or target before leaving the safe point? Is there a safe position that is too stable and costs nothing?

Light and shadow: Does the darkness provide information and suspense, rather than simply hindering recognition? Does blocking light make a space more mysterious rather than more confusing to navigate?

Materials: Can area hazards be pre-read through materials, colors, sounds, and surface conditions? Can players feel like they are moving from a safe world into a dangerous one?

Height: Is the high point contestable and counterable? Are the dangers of cliffs and pits clear, understandable, and avoidable?

This set of checks does not replace enemy design, mission design, or numerical balance, but it can help level designers identify problems earlier in the gray box phase. Many levels fail not because there are few routes, but because players cannot judge why they are nervous, why they are safe, and why it is worth moving forward.

## 10. Supplementary inspiration for search, fight, withdrawal and action levels

For search-and-defense, PvPvE, stealth shooter, melee ARPG, or indoor exploration games, this article is particularly inspiring.

The extraction point should have both a prospect and a shelter relationship. Players need to see the withdrawal commitment, but they can't have a completely safe settlement button. Evacuation points should be surrounded by multiple entry and exit routes, anti-squatting structures, sound penalty, or line of sight obstruction.

High value rooms should have entry scripts. The front entrance can be fast but noisy, the side entrance can be slow but discreet, and the vertical entrance can be strong but at the cost of exposure. Players are not simply "opening the door to get the goods", but choosing a risk.

Boss rooms and high-value areas shouldn’t just be bigger. They should change the player's physical feel: more open, more exposed, and less secure, while giving clear attack rhythm and the promise of retreat.

The outer low-risk areas should also not just fill space. They can provide recovery, intelligence, low-value gains, detours, and secondary entry opportunities. Without the protection of the outer circle, players are left with two choices: rush for hot spots or give up.
